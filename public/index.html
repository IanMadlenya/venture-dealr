Investors Dilute

You've convinced investors to give you seed money. Investment dilutes.

Your investors assign a value to your company (the pre-money), and their percentage stake is how much they put in relative to your pre-money.

However, you also created an options pool to give equity to new hires. This is counted as part of the pre-money.

...but Assign Value

Instead of looking at percentage, we can look at the value of your equity.

Note that your company had no value until your first valuation event. In percentage terms you were diluted, but in value terms you have something instead of nothing!

The (Empty Handed) Exit

Liquidation preferences reduce risk for later investors.

Investors with seniority get their money out first, and only if there's anything left do others get a piece. If you're near the back, your claim may even be underwater -- it may be worth less than you initially put in.

If you're in the back of the line (such as common stock), you may even walk away with nothing if there's not enough to satisfy the claims in front of you.

A Positive Exit With Nothing

Even a positive exit can still leave you with nothing.

Because the exit amount is subject to preferences, this pie is not split evenly. Some investors may make their money back (plus an extra profit), but the extra profit may not be enough to match the value of common-stock holders.

In fact, if the common stock is in the form of options, the holder would have to pay more than their stakes were worth in order to exercise the options. The holder would instead just walk away with nothing.
